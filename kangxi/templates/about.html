A method of ordering Chinese characters using structural sequences of their components

Boris Klimenko

The issue of ordering Chinese characters in dictionaries remains problematic. Despite the fact that most characters contain regularly occurring components consisting of several elements, there have been no ordering methods that would be able to group characters with common components together and automate such ordering with the help of the standard sorting procedure. This project aims at providing such a method.

A character can consist of two types of components: radicals and other characters. Radicals (Kangxi character keys) comprise a set of 214 characters, arranged in ascending order of the number of strokes. In addition to the standard style adopted in the Kangxi dictionary, many radicals have several other variants used in individual characters. For example, the standard style radical 人 can be represented in characters as 亻, the radical 乙 as 乚, the radical 手 as 扌, etc. However, the structural sequences used for character ordering (discussed below) employ only standard variants of radicals, while alternative styles are used only in characters themselves. The complete list of standard and nonstandard options of radical styles is given in the Appendix. Radicals, together with their variants, are assumed here to be the smallest indivisible unit of the structure of any character.

The structural sequence of a character is the combination of radicals and characters included in it ordered in the manner described below.

Characters with the simplest structure contain only two radicals. The structural sequence of the components of a two-radical character consists of the two radicals included in it in their standard style and the character itself. The character itself is included in the sequence in order to differentiate the structural sequences of different characters consisting of the same radicals, for example:

丿丿乂 vs. 丿丿𠂆

一木未 vs. 一木末 vs. 一木本

In two-radical characters with horizontal or vertical arrangement of radicals, the left or the bottom radical is assumed to be the first element of the structural sequence, while the right or top radical is assumed to be the second one.

When a character is formed through insertion of one radical into another (e.g. 冂 一 𠔼, 囗 靑 圊) or their intersection (e.g. 囗 丨 中, 亠 冂 丹, 二 冂 冄), the following rule applies. The first radical of the sequence is the radical that forms the leftmost or bottom part of the character (with priority given to the left side). If the top part of either of the radicals is at the top of the character, this radical is the second one: (e.g. 一 木 未, 一 木 末, 一 木本). Radicals located within a closed radical are placed second (e.g. 卩 丶 卪, 囗 人 囚, 尸 丨 𠃜). Radicals with an open right bottom part go first in the sequence (e.g. 尸 二 𡰥, 广 匕 庀, 疒 匕 疕, 疒 矢 疾). Radicals that do not have an open bottom part in their standard style but partially compress in a manner that creates such a part when combined with another radical are placed second in the sequence (e.g. 火 鹿 麃, 石 麻 磨, 禾 鹿 麇).

Decomposition of most two-radical characters into component radicals is straightforward. Ambiguous cases are resolved with the help of the rare variants of radical styles, whose complete list is given in the Appendix.

The algorithm presented above orders 100% of the characters. The place of each character is determined by the order of the radicals (radical numbers) in their structural sequences sorted together (for two-radical roots, including two-radical single characters) and roots having 3 or more radicals (including single characters).

The ordering using structural sequences reveals several features of the entire set of characters.

A group of characters that makes up 7.4% of the total number of characters (763 out of 10181) consists of characters that are included as a common element in 89% of all characters (characters of this group are part of one to over 200 other characters). Characters from this group are referred to as “roots” hereafter. Sequences of characters including any of such roots are labelled as “trees”.

This way of ordering characters is different from the existing methods employed in dictionaries. In dictionaries built according to the Russian graphic system, for example (Oshanin 1983), characters are ordered by the type of the element located in the bottom right corner of the character. This method does not allow to group characters with a common element together, thus overlooking the tree-like structure of such character groups, which, when ordered by their structural sequences, represent trees having a trunk root, trunk, branch roots, and branches.

In addition, such groups in (Oshanin 1983) do not include all the characters that include the common element, because they contain only characters with the same stroke in the bottom right corner: e.g. the sequence 酋 崷 鞧 輶 猶 蕕 楢 緧 鰌 蝤 contains characters that include the character 酋 in (Oshanin 1983, vol. 2: 705–706). The remaining characters that contain the same character as their common element are scattered across different volumes and pages of the dictionary:

Tree structures are formed automatically when characters are ordered according to their structural sequences. In the illustrations of the tree composition for all the roots given in this description, the trees are shown in the form of lists of characters without highlighting the branches (the branches are included in the tree trunks directly behind the roots of the branches). The following is an illustration of a linear structure of a tree with branches enclosed in square brackets:

Structural sequences allow to place each character in its unique position within a tree, as well as make search for any character in trees of any size faster. This leads to the possibility to significantly improve the usability of printed dictionaries and simplify the algorithms in their electronic implementation.

Basing a dictionary on roots ordered by their structural sequences allows to improve the structure of printed dictionaries, and organize their table of contents in a more convenient form. This can greatly improve the whole process of character search. Examples of such a table of contents are given above. Single characters that are not included in any others are referred to as single roots (single pairs — in the case of a two-radical root). They are also included in the general sequence of roots in the dictionary table of contents.

Both in electronic and printed dictionaries, this root ordering method allows to arrange all roots (both forming trees and single) in a matrix, (given that the vast majority of such roots consist of two radicals), which is the most rational structure in this case from the point of view of ease of processing and perception.

The roots are placed in a 214x214 matrix (total number of the radicals). The row and column indices in the matrix are the radicals ordered in ascending order of their numbers. Each root in the matrix is either associated with a corresponding tree or is a single.

The root matrix allows to arrange 100% of characters to be included in a dictionary, regardless of their total number.

The position of roots in the matrix is based on their structural sequences. For two-radical roots, the first radical defines the row, while the second one defines the column. Roots consisting of more than two radicals are placed in several matrix cells depending on the number of their radicals that are pairwise adjacent to each other. For example, the root 眞 with the structural sequence 八 乙 目 匕 眞 is placed in three cells: 12 八 - 5 乙, 5 乙 - 109 目 and 109 目 - 21 匕, where the number stands for the radical number.

The order of radicals within a structural sequence can be different. The first radical in the sequence may be the leading radical of the root. It is possible to put in the first place the radical located in the top or rightmost part of the character. Any other conditions for their location can be added in the case of insertion or intersection of radicals or when there are three or more radicals in the root. However, all these modifications will only change the distribution of roots in the matrix, without changing the composition and structure of trees. But they will violate the natural logic of constructing structural sequences of roots, which locates the roots along the rows and columns of the matrix and does not require additional information about leading radicals of each character.
